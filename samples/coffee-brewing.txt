A Practical Guide to Brewing Coffee at Home

Coffee brewing is the process of extracting soluble flavor compounds from roasted, ground coffee beans using hot water. Although the chemistry is complex, a handful of variables control most of the outcome: the grind size, the water temperature, the brew time, and the ratio of coffee to water. Master those four and almost any method — pour-over, French press, espresso, or cold brew — becomes repeatable.

Grind size matters because it determines how much surface area the water touches. A fine grind extracts quickly and suits short brew methods like espresso, which forces water through the coffee bed in under thirty seconds. A coarse grind extracts slowly and suits immersion methods like the French press, where the grounds steep for four minutes or more. If your coffee tastes sour and thin, the extraction was likely too weak: grind finer or brew longer. If it tastes bitter and harsh, the extraction went too far: grind coarser or shorten the brew.

Water temperature is the second lever. The generally accepted range is 90 to 96 degrees Celsius. Cooler water under-extracts, emphasizing acidity; hotter water risks scalding the grounds and pulling out bitter compounds. Cold brew sidesteps temperature entirely by steeping coarse grounds in room-temperature or refrigerated water for twelve to twenty-four hours, producing a smooth, low-acid concentrate.

The brew ratio ties everything together. A common starting point for filter coffee is one gram of coffee for every sixteen grams of water — roughly 60 grams of coffee per litre. Espresso runs much stronger, typically one part coffee to two parts liquid in the cup. Ratios are a starting point, not a rule; taste and adjust.

Freshness is the variable people most often ignore. Roasted coffee releases carbon dioxide for weeks after roasting, and its aromatic compounds fade quickly once the bean is ground. Whole beans stored in an airtight container away from light keep well for about a month after the roast date. Pre-ground coffee begins to taste flat within days. If you make one upgrade to your routine, buy a burr grinder and grind just before brewing.

Finally, water quality deserves a mention. Coffee is more than 98 percent water, so heavily chlorinated or very hard water will dominate the cup. Filtered tap water is fine for most people; distilled water is actually too pure and produces flat, dull coffee because some mineral content is needed to carry flavor.
